# Multilingual website in Romanian, English and German: when it is worth it and how to organise it

A multilingual website can open doors to customers, partners or funders who do not speak the company's main language. But automatically translating every page is not enough.

Romanian, English and German can serve different audiences. Sometimes you need the same pages in all languages. Other times, each language needs adapted messages, examples and depth.

## Why this matters in practice

A poorly organised multilingual site creates confusion: inconsistent menus, missing pages, literal translations, weak SEO and content that is hard to update. A well-organised one helps the user reach the right language and information quickly.

## Questions the page needs to answer

A visitor does not read a website like a brochure. Most people arrive with a few practical questions in mind:

- Who is each language for?
- Should all pages be translated or only the important ones?
- Are there differences in offer, market or tone between languages?
- How will users switch language on the site?
- Who updates the content in each language?

## What the website should include

A good website does not need to be overloaded. It needs to be complete in the places that matter:

- Clear URL structure for each language, such as /ro/, /en/, /de/.
- A language switcher and navigation that are easy to find.
- Localised copy, not word-for-word translation.
- Meta title, meta description and SEO content adapted for each language.
- An update process so a Romanian change does not remain untranslated in English or German.

## Common mistakes

Most problems do not come from a lack of design. They come from unclear information. These are the mistakes worth avoiding:

- Using automatic translation without human review.
- Mixing languages on the same page.
- Having important pages in one language and missing in another without a reason.
- Not adapting examples for international audiences.
- Forgetting technical SEO for multilingual sites.

## What to prepare before starting

Before asking for a quote or starting a redesign, prepare a few basic things:

- Audiences for each language.
- List of pages worth translating.
- Glossary of terms: services, products, names, tone.
- Rules for URLs, menus and duplicate content.
- Owner for translations and updates.

## How to know whether it works

A website should not be judged only by how it looks on launch day. Track whether people reach the important pages, understand the offer and send clear enquiries.

Track traffic and conversions separately by language. If visitors in one language leave quickly or do not enquire, the issue may be translation, offer, lack of relevant proof or navigation.

## Conclusion

A multilingual website is worth it when you have real audiences in several languages. The key is organisation: structure, localisation, SEO and maintenance.
