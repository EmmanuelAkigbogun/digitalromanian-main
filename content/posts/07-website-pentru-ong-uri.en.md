# Website for NGOs: credibility, donations and projects presented clearly

For an NGO, the website is more than a digital business card. It is the place where a donor, volunteer, partner or funder checks whether the organisation is real, active and coherent.

People need more than emotion. They need to understand the issue, what the organisation actually does, what results exist and how they can contribute without searching through several channels.

## Why this matters in practice

In the nonprofit sector, trust is built through transparency. Projects, reports, team, partners and donation mechanisms need to be visible. A confusing website can make a serious organisation look inactive or difficult to verify.

## Questions the page needs to answer

A visitor does not read a website like a brochure. Most people arrive with a few practical questions in mind:

- What is the mission, explained simply?
- Which projects are active and what results did past projects have?
- How can someone donate or become a volunteer?
- Are reports, partners, funders or public documents available?
- Who is behind the organisation and how can people contact it?

## What the website should include

A good website does not need to be overloaded. It needs to be complete in the places that matter:

- Clear pages for mission, projects, impact, team and contact.
- A donation section with simple steps and an explanation of how funds are used.
- Reports, documents, press releases or relevant materials organised by year or project.
- Stories and concrete examples, not only generic statements about change.
- Separate forms for volunteers, partnerships, press and donations.

## Common mistakes

Most problems do not come from a lack of design. They come from unclear information. These are the mistakes worth avoiding:

- Speaking only about values without showing activity.
- Hiding important documents in PDFs that are hard to find.
- Not updating projects after they end.
- Using the same form for every type of contact.
- Not explaining what happens after someone donates or signs up.

## What to prepare before starting

Before asking for a quote or starting a redesign, prepare a few basic things:

- List active projects and archive completed ones.
- Collect measurable results: beneficiaries, activities, materials, areas covered.
- Prepare transparency documents and public reports.
- Write clear messages for donors, volunteers and partners.
- Assign one person responsible for content updates.

## How to know whether it works

A website should not be judged only by how it looks on launch day. Track whether people reach the important pages, understand the offer and send clear enquiries.

Track donations, volunteer signups, partnership messages, document downloads and visits to project pages. For an NGO, the website works when it turns interest into practical involvement.

## Conclusion

An NGO website should feel human, but also verifiable. The mission attracts attention, while clarity, projects and transparency build trust.
