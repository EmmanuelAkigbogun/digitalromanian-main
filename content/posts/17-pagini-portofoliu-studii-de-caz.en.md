# Portfolio pages and case studies: how to prove you can deliver

Any company can say it works professionally. A good portfolio shows it without forcing the message. Case studies explain not only what was delivered, but why it was needed, how the work happened and what changed.

For a customer comparing suppliers, proof is more persuasive than promises. A well-built portfolio page reduces perceived risk and helps the customer imagine a similar project.

## Why this matters in practice

A portfolio is not just an image gallery. It is a sales and credibility tool. The clearer you explain context, constraints and results, the easier it is for the customer to understand the value.

## Questions the page needs to answer

A visitor does not read a website like a brochure. Most people arrive with a few practical questions in mind:

- Who was the project for?
- What was the problem or objective?
- What solution was delivered?
- Which steps mattered in the process?
- What result, feedback or indicator can be mentioned?

## What the website should include

A good website does not need to be overloaded. It needs to be complete in the places that matter:

- A project list with title, industry, service type and short result.
- Detailed case studies for important projects.
- Images, screenshots, photos or before/after materials.
- Process and decision explanations, not only the final result.
- Testimonials or short quotes, when permission exists.

## Common mistakes

Most problems do not come from a lack of design. They come from unclear information. These are the mistakes worth avoiding:

- Showing only images without context.
- Exaggerating results without proof.
- Not explaining your role in the project.
- Hiding real constraints, even though they make the case more credible.
- Not updating the portfolio after new projects.

## What to prepare before starting

Before asking for a quote or starting a redesign, prepare a few basic things:

- List projects you are allowed to publish.
- Approved visual materials.
- Details about objective, process and deliverables.
- Measurable results or relevant feedback.
- Client approval when names or images are sensitive.

## How to know whether it works

A website should not be judged only by how it looks on launch day. Track whether people reach the important pages, understand the offer and send clear enquiries.

Track how many visitors view the portfolio before submitting a form and which projects are viewed most often. Good case studies influence lead quality, not only traffic.

## Conclusion

A good portfolio does not brag without substance. It explains the situation, shows the work and proves that the promise can be delivered.
